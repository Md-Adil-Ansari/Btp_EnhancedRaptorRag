The quarterly meeting of the Regional Culinary Board was focused on a dispute between two local bakeries regarding the "Crisp-Factor" of their respective sourdough loaves. Bakery A claimed that Bakery B had stolen a specific fermentation timing sequence that was proprietary to their brand. To settle the matter, the board’s mediator required a full audit of the kitchen logs from both parties. Because the bakeries wished to protect their secret flour ratios from the public, they invoked a specific protective order for the discovery phase. To execute the Protocol-22 Evidence Sequestration, the lead auditor must first serve a Notice of Impoundment to the digital custodian of the facility. Once the delivery is confirmed via a blockchain timestamp, the auditor must apply a dual-key encryption lock to the metadata drive. This procedure ensures that the raw data remains untouched and invisible to unauthorized third parties until the final hearing. It is mandatory to maintain the integrity of the physical seal until the mediator grants a 'Clear-to-View' status. Failure to adhere to this protocol results in the immediate dismissal of the infringement claim and the automatic release of the impounded data back to the originating party. In this low-stakes bakery feud, the "release of data" simply meant the secret fermentation time was no longer protected. The mediator dismissed the case, the bakeries went back to selling bread, and the local culinary market remained unaffected. No significant financial or social damage occurred.
